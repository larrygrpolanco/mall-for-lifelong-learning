Mobile-Assisted Language Learning (MALL)
MALL is simply defined as “the use of mobile technology in language learning” (Miangah & Nezarat, 2012, p. 311)

The portability of mobile devices provides more diverse ways of learning languages, allowing personalized learning opportunities that traditional classroom settings may not easily provide (e.g., Kukulska-Hulme, 2009; Thornton & Houser, 2005).

Among various types of technologies, commercial MALL apps have been examined in terms of the effectiveness across a variety of learner contexts. 
UNIVERSITY OF SOUTH FLORIDA
While definitions vary, Mobile-Assisted Language Learning, or MALL is broadly defined as the use of mobile technology in language learning. 
Its primary advantage is portability, allowing learners to engage with the language anytime and anywhere, extending learning well beyond the classroom. 
While various technologies exist, commercial MALL apps like Duolingo have received significant attention due to their global availability and accessibility.

Current study
Despite the rapid increase of global older adult population (United Nations, 2020), their specific experiences with MALL remains under-researched. 

The current study examines older adults’ (individuals aged 60 and above) engagement with Duolingo in informal learning environments, focusing on three constructs: user experience (UX), motivation, and metacognition.

This research gap is critical given the rapid increase of the global older adult population. 
Therefore, this study examines older adults’ engagement with Duolingo in informal learning environments. I specifically focused on three constructs, User Experience (UX), Motivation, and Metacognition.

RQs
RQ1: What is the nature of older adults’ UX (e.g., attitudes, perceived benefits and drawbacks) when using Duolingo for language learning in informal settings? To what extent does their UX change over time? 

RQ2: How do older adults utilize Duolingo in terms of self-monitoring practices? 

RQ3: What motivational factors influence older adults to choose Duolingo initially (i.e., entering motivation) and to continue using it over time (i.e., task motivation)? 
Based on this model, I formulated three research questions: 
RQ1 examines user experience, which can be defined as the participants’ attitudes and perceptions, specifically the benefits and drawbacks they perceived while using the app. I also explored how this UX evolved over time, based on Garrison’s argument that these constructs are dynamic and constantly influencing one another. 
RQ2 investigates self-monitoring strategies. Here, I examined the specific strategies older adults employ to manage their learning, focusing on their metacognition and metacognitive awareness. 
and RQ3 explores motivation. Drawing on Garrison’s framework, I divided this into two distinct categories. First, I examined Entering motivation, which is the initial driving force that led them to choose Duolingo for language learning. Second, I looked at task motivation, which is the sustained effort and volition required to continue their language learning journey.

General Discussion and Implications
 Garrison’s (1997) SDL model allowed for the understanding of self-management not as an isolated act, but as the interplay of resources, context, metacognition, and motivation.
 
 High persistence and consistency show that older adults are competent, active digital learners. 

 Their unique preference (e.g., repetition, cognitive health) highlights the importance of older adults as a vital, yet under recognized, demographic for MALL research and industry. 
UNIVERSITY OF SOUTH FLORIDA
Three major implications emerge from this study. 
Regarding my theoretical framework, Garrison’s model provided a holistic understanding of older adults’ language learning experience, as it prompted me to view their learning not as isolated acts but as a cumulative result of resources, context, and motivation. 
And we found that learner competence in this demographic. The persistence and consistency observed demonstrates that older adults are competent, active digital learners. 
It leads to the importance of this demographic. Their unique preferences, such as the focus on cognitive health, and preference for repetition, highlight them as a vital demographic for both SLA research and the MALL industry. 

Limitations and Future Research Directions
Recruitment of current Duolingo users might have favored intrinsically motivated learners; future research might consider targeting novice users. 

Recruitment challenges specific to this age group included wariness of online fraud and unfamiliarity with tools (e.g., Qualtrics).

The use of structured, prompted reflective journals potentially influenced participants’ metacognitive reflection; less intrusive methods are recommended. 
Finally, I should address the limitations of this study, which offer important directions for future research. 
First, recruiting current users likely favored intrinsically motivated learners. Futures studies should target novice users as well. 
Second, I encountered recruitment barriers due to older adults’ fears of online fraud.  Many older adults were hesitant to click survey link. Reflecting on this, I realized that while Qualtrics is a standard tool for us in academia, to an older adult outside the university setting, an unfamiliar URL looks suspicious. This highlights a critical need for building trust and maybe utilizing different recruitment channels rather than relying solely on digital distribution. 
Lastly, using structured journal prompts may have influenced the data. One participant, Jung-ja, explicitly mentioned that the prompts ‘made her think.’ While this enhanced her learning, it may have influenced the ecological validity. Future research might consider less intrusive methods, such as screen recording with think-aloud protocols.
