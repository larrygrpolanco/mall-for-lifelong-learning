## 2. THE THREE BIG TAKEAWAYS (Per Jihye)

1. **Various Learning Strategies** — Older adults aren't passive app users. They employ sophisticated, diverse self-monitoring strategies (writing things down, repeating lessons, seeking external resources, self-evaluating) and actively adapt their learning approaches over time.

2. **High Motivation** — Motivation levels were consistently high and *stable* throughout the entire study — no drop-off. This challenges the common narrative that app-based learners lose interest over time. Their motivation was mostly intrinsic: leisure, cognitive health, family bonding.

3. **Need for Repetition** — Unlike younger learners who often criticize repetitive content, older adults *valued* repetition as a core learning strategy. They actively sought it out, went back to redo lessons, and viewed it as essential for both language learning and brain health.

## 3. THE PROBLEM / WHY THIS MATTERS

### The Gap
- MALL research has overwhelmingly focused on young adults (undergrads) in formal classroom settings
- Over 800 million people use Duolingo globally across all age groups
- Only ~2 prior studies have specifically examined older adults (60+) using language learning apps
- The world's population is aging rapidly (Pew Research Center, 2024)
- Lifelong learning offers cognitive, social, and community benefits for older adults

### Why Older Adults?
- They learn differently — different behavioral patterns AND different brain regions activated during language learning (Marcotte & Ansaldo, 2014)
- They perceive and adopt technology differently (Kuo et al., 2012; Farage et al., 2012)
- Language learning may help prevent cognitive decline — and older adults *know this* and cite it as motivation
- MALL's portability removes physical barriers (no commuting to classrooms, no tuition)
- Older adults may actually be *better* at sustaining self-directed learning than younger populations

### The Counter-Narrative
This research directly challenges **deficit-based narratives** about older adults and technology. The participants were not passive, confused, or disengaged — they were competent, strategic, persistent, and self-aware.

---

## 4. RESEARCH QUESTIONS

**RQ1 (User Experience):** What is the nature of older adults' UX (attitudes, perceived benefits and drawbacks) when using Duolingo for language learning in informal settings? To what extent does their UX change over time?

**RQ2 (Self-Monitoring / Metacognition):** How do older adults utilize Duolingo in terms of self-monitoring practices?

**RQ3 (Motivation):** What motivational factors influence older adults to choose Duolingo initially (entering motivation) and to continue using it over time (task motivation)?

---

## 5. THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK

**Garrison's (1997) Self-Directed Learning (SDL) Model**

Three interconnected constructs:
- **Self-Management** → In this context, the app itself is the primary resource (UX matters here)
- **Self-Monitoring** → Metacognitive strategies: how learners assess, reflect, and adjust
- **Motivation** → Entering motivation (why start) + Task motivation (why keep going)

These three constructs are *not isolated* — they dynamically interact. A learner's UX shapes their motivation, which shapes how they monitor their learning, which feeds back into how they use the app.

## 7. FINDINGS — RQ1: USER EXPERIENCE

### Headline
Older adults maintained **consistently positive and stable** attitudes toward Duolingo throughout the 10-week study. No statistically significant changes between pre- and post-questionnaires. However, they were also *discerning* — identifying specific benefits and drawbacks.

### Key Quantitative Data
- All UX items scored high on 7-point scale (lowest was 4.7)
- **Recommendation willingness** scored highest: Pre M=5.8, Post M=6.3
- No significant temporal changes (paired t-tests, Wilcoxon tests)
- Effect sizes all small — attitudes remained stable
- Notable: the effect size for recommendation willingness (r = -0.6) suggests a meaningful (though not statistically significant) increase — sustained use *strengthened* endorsement

### Perceived Benefits (from thematic analysis)

**1. General MALL Benefits (14/15 participants)**
- Availability / learn anytime anywhere (8 mentions)
- Gamification / game-like elements (8 mentions)
- Personalized learning (5 mentions)
- Free / low cost (4 mentions)

**2. Instruction Format — Positive (13/15)**
- **Repetition valued** (7 mentions) — KEY FINDING: younger learners criticize this, older adults embrace it
- Overall format well-structured (6 mentions)
- Interactive elements like cheering friends (5 mentions)
- Instant feedback (3 mentions)

**3. Helpful for Language Learning (10/15)**
- Listening skills (6) — most appreciated linguistic area
- Grammar (5)
- Overall language development (3)
- Speaking (3)
- Vocabulary (3)
- Writing (2)

**4. Helpful for Brain Health (4/15)**
- Used Duolingo specifically for cognitive maintenance, not just language
- "As I approach the age of 73, Duolingo helps to keep my mind sharp" (P1)
- Often linked repetition → memory improvement → brain health

### Perceived Drawbacks

**1. Instruction Format — Negative (13/15)**
- **Lack of explanation** (9) — biggest complaint; grammar rules not explained
- Lack of practices/stories (5)
- Lack of authentic/cultural input (4)
- Instruction order/pace too fast (3)
- Over-emphasis on grammar (2)

**2. Functions & Features — Negative (7/15)**
- Errors and mistakes / voice recognition issues (5)
- Unattractive features (3)

**3. Free Version Limits (5/15)**
- Overwhelming ads (4)
- Limited access / heart system (3)

**4. Lack of Customer Service (3/15)**
- Described as "non-existent"
- No phone support — a demographic-specific need
- "There is no number to call when you have a problem" (P7)

### Case Study Highlights — RQ1

**Sung-jin (68, Korean, retired):** Used Duolingo primarily for cognitive health. Lives in rural Korea with no practical need for English. Appreciated repetition, gamification, interactive elements. Connected with family/friends on the app. "Duolingo has a lot more advantages than disadvantages."

**Alison (66, Canadian radiologist):** Long-time paying subscriber (tried free, Super, and Max). Loved short session format for her unpredictable schedule. Valued AI speaking feature. But: criticized pace, lack of explanation, insufficient practice. Used external resources (online courses, dictionaries) to compensate. "They don't really ask for feedback directly... because they could make their program even better."

## 8. FINDINGS — RQ2: SELF-MONITORING (METACOGNITION)

### Headline
Older adults demonstrated **high metacognitive awareness** and employed **7 distinct self-monitoring strategies** (out of Zimmerman's 15-category framework). They were not passive users — they actively managed, evaluated, and adapted their learning.

### The 7 Strategies Found

**1. Self-Evaluating (14/15 participants)** — Most prevalent
- Growth/Improvement (10): Positive self-assessments, noting progress
- Frustration/Feeling of ineffectiveness (9): Honest about challenges
- Finding personalized strategies (8): Increased over time — learning how to learn
- Age-related evaluations (5): Initially prominent, decreased over time → shift from age-focused to learning-focused identity

**2. Rehearsing & Memorizing (12/15)**
- Writing down (9): Prevalent traditional strategy — writing vocab by hand
- Speaking production (6): Practicing aloud, increased over time

**3. Goal-Setting & Planning (12/15)**
- Goals relied on Duolingo features (9): Streaks, daily quests, leaderboards
- General/personal goals (8): Evolved from simple time-based → long-term and skill-specific

**4. Keeping Records & Monitoring (11/15)**
- Electronic/physical notes (6): Personal notebooks, tracking units/dates
- Duolingo features (4): Declined over time — app's built-in tracking perceived as insufficient

**5. Self-Consequating (9/15)**
- Imagination of rewards/success (9): Envisioning real-world communication, travel conversations
- Zero instances of self-punishment — exclusively positive

**6. Organizing & Transforming (8/15)**
- Repetition (8): Deliberately going back to redo earlier lessons — strategic, not accidental
- Ranged from macro (entire levels) to micro (repeating single audio clips)

**7. Seeking Information (7/15)**
- Seeking input (6): Soap operas, music, other apps for more language exposure
- Seeking explanation (4): Google, ChatGPT, dictionaries to fill gaps Duolingo leaves

### Key Temporal Patterns
- **Age-related concerns decreased** over time (prominent in Entry 1, faded by Entry 3)
- **Personalized strategies increased** over time (barely present in Entry 1, prominent in Entry 3)
- This suggests: reflective journaling itself may have promoted metacognitive development
- Shift from "I'm too old for this" → "Here's my specific strategy for improvement"

### Case Study Highlights — RQ2

**Jung-ja (78, Korean, elementary education):** The researcher's grandmother. Started Duolingo on her granddaughter's recommendation. Initial strategy: write everything down + repeat. Over time: recognized grammar was too hard → pivoted to vocabulary-focused approach → downloaded a second vocabulary app. Learned to use Naver (Korean search engine) independently to look things up. Pride in digital literacy growth: "I think it's a kind of energy I get... now I know how to look up things you don't know." Reflective journaling "made me think."

**Dale (70, American, retired nurse):** German speaker learning Spanish for 5 years. Already had well-established strategies before the study. Used Duolingo as "a TOOL" — supplemented with soap operas (watched in English first, then Spanish), grammar textbooks, Google searches, and external proficiency tests (Instituto Cervantes). Rejected Duolingo's built-in progress tracking as unhelpful. Traveled to Costa Rica during the study and successfully communicated in Spanish. Represents what "mature self-directed learning" looks like.

## 9. FINDINGS — RQ3: MOTIVATION

### Headline
Participants showed **consistently high and stable motivation** throughout the study, with no statistically significant decline. This directly challenges findings with younger populations who often show motivation drop-off and high attrition.

### Key Quantitative Data
- All 5 motivation items scored very high on 7-point scale
- **Learning enjoyment** highest in pre-questionnaire (M = 6.4)
- **Maintenance of learning effort** highest in post-questionnaire (M = 6.5)
- **Learner beliefs** (self-efficacy) consistently lowest — high motivation coexists with lower confidence
- No significant changes over time — motivation was rock-solid

### Entering Motivation (Why They Started)

**1. Leisure (11/15)** — Most common
- Travel (7 participants): "may travel one day"
- Language learning as hobby/intellectual pursuit
- Some studied multiple languages for fun: "Arabic-for fun Klingon-I'm a Star Trek nerd" (P9)

**2. Cognitive Maintenance (5/15)**
- Explicitly to prevent dementia or keep mind sharp
- 4/5 specifically mentioned "preventing dementia"
- Health-oriented motivation unique to this demographic

**3. Functional Application (4/15)**
- Practical daily use — especially Korean speakers navigating English signage
- "Relieve frustration in daily life (English environment)" (P4)

**4. Social/Family Bonding (4/15)**
- Heritage language connection
- Learning partner's/son-in-law's language
- Community language

**5. Acculturation (2/15)**
- Planning to move abroad: "Might be moving to Italy" (P1), "I want to retire to Spain" (P9)

### Task Motivation (Why They Kept Going)

**Positive Factors:**

**1. Gamified Elements (8/15)** — Strongest sustaining factor
- Leaderboards (5): competition, league maintenance
- Monthly badges (2)
- Characters / mascot encouragement (Young-cheol)
- Complex relationship: motivates engagement but can also reduce effort once demotion is avoided

**2. Social Accountability (2/15)**
- Family/friends connected on Duolingo
- "Learning continuously with people you know motivates you to learn" (P2)
- Leveraged existing offline relationships, not new online communities

**3. Study-Influenced Commitment (2/15)**
- Participation in research itself was motivating — external accountability helped
- Suggests older adults may benefit from structured engagement like learning communities

**Negative Factor:**

**4. Social Priorities & Temporal Conflicts (3/15)**
- Life got in the way: caregiving, work, other responsibilities
- "I couldn't do it today because I didn't have time to study" (P3)

### Case Study Highlights — RQ3

**Young-cheol (83, Korean, elementary education):** Jung-ja's husband. Motivated by functional application (reading English signs) and cognitive maintenance (preventing dementia). At 83, averaged 34 min/session across 41 active days — *exceeding* the Duolingo average. Total: 1,410 minutes over 9 weeks. Uniquely motivated by app characters (Duo the owl) rather than leaderboards. Could recognize English words in daily life: "dates," "rice," "gate" — small victories that reinforced his learning.

**Ken (64, American, PhD in computer science):** 10-year Duolingo veteran. Motivated by leisure, cognitive maintenance, and family bonding (sister is fluent Spanish speaker). Strategic user: spent only ~10 min/session but earned the most XP (5,440 total) through optimization. Openly aware that gamification metrics don't equal proficiency: "I mean, none of this really matters in the real sense, but still my brain tells me, hey, you're in the Diamond League." Described language learning as "like a puzzle."

## 10. GENERAL DISCUSSION — 6 KEY IMPLICATIONS

### 1. Higher Persistence Than Younger Learners
Older adults showed greater temporal consistency, stable positive attitudes, sustained motivation, and very low attrition (1/16 dropped out). This may stem from: greater life stability, fewer competing obligations, enhanced self-regulation developed over decades, more intrinsic motivation. **Takeaway for the field:** Older adults are not fringe users — they are high-retention consumers worthy of targeted design.

### 2. The Confidence Gap
Despite demonstrating sophisticated learning strategies and persistent engagement, older adults had relatively low self-efficacy beliefs. They *are* competent but often *don't believe* they are. Age-related stereotypes were internalized. **Takeaway:** Apps should integrate frequent positive reinforcement and clear progress visualization to scaffold confidence.

### 3. Sophisticated Metacognition
Participants employed diverse self-monitoring strategies, identified app limitations, and actively supplemented with external resources. They were *not* passive technology consumers. **Takeaway:** Don't oversimplify interfaces for older adults — provide robust, flexible tools that respect their intelligence.

### 4. Repetition & Cognitive Health (Age-Specific)
Two patterns unique to this demographic: (a) strong preference for repetition (positive, not a complaint) and (b) health-oriented motivation where cognitive maintenance is a primary driver. **Takeaway:** Apps targeting older adults should embrace recursive learning structures and explicitly position cognitive maintenance as a core value.

### 5. Customer Service Gap
Older adults need synchronous, human-mediated support (phone calls, real-time help), not just FAQs and emails. Current support was perceived as "non-existent." **Takeaway:** Responsive, accessible support is critical for user retention in this demographic.

### 6. Garrison's SDL Model Works
The theoretical framework successfully captured the interplay of UX, metacognition, and motivation in informal MALL. The case study approach revealed how personal/social background shapes all three constructs. **Takeaway:** Future MALL research should adopt holistic frameworks that account for context, not just isolated variables.

## 11. LIMITATIONS TO ACKNOWLEDGE

- Participants were *active* Duolingo users → possible selection bias toward already-motivated individuals
- Reflective journaling may have itself promoted metacognition (the instrument was also an intervention)
- No measurement of actual linguistic gains (study focused on experience, not proficiency outcomes)
- L1 backgrounds limited to Korean and English
- Small sample size (N=15), though appropriate for qualitative case study approach
